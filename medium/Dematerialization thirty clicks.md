# Dematerialization: thirty clicks and a hundred and fifty fates

**Autore:** Calogero Kàlos Bonasia  
**Pubblicazione:** Medium  
**Licenza:** CC BY-ND 4.0  

---

You configured a workflow thinking you were ordering coffee. Someone was counting on exactly that.

---

The journey from mainframe to personal computer, from personal computer to smartphone, from smartphone to cloud, from command line to touchscreen, describes a progressive dematerialization that is easy to mistake for costless progress.

Computers have become invisible, ubiquitous, embedded in everyday objects. And this ubiquity has produced, as every apparent miracle does, a corresponding illusion: the idea that technology has finally become natural, that the interface has dissolved the distance between human intention and machine response, that pressing a button is the same as understanding what the button sets in motion.

That distance has been made invisible, which is a profoundly different thing. What is invisible cannot be questioned, challenged, understood.

Anyone who worked with machines when machines occupied entire rooms knows this: those systems imposed a humility that no contemporary interface demands. Accessing them required protocols, expertise, mediation. You knew you were dealing with something larger than your comprehension, and that awareness kept you vigilant, cautious, honest about the limits of what you understood.

Then someone decided that humility was an obstacle to sales.

---

## Warranty void if removed

The information systems that govern today's organizations, Jira, SAP, Salesforce, ServiceNow, are enormously more complex than any mainframe from the 1970s. But their complexity has been carefully buried under layers of graphical interfaces, color-coded dashboards, and guided procedures that promise configuration in three easy steps. As if the simplicity of the gesture guaranteed the simplicity of what the gesture produces.

It is the same logic as the tamper-proof seal: they prevent you from opening the hood because, if you opened it, you would see how the engine actually works. And whoever sees how the engine works starts asking uncomfortable questions.

I remember the day I watched a senior manager configure a workflow on a project management platform. It took maybe thirty clicks, performed with the ease of someone ordering coffee. Those thirty clicks defined the workflow of a hundred and fifty people, determined who could approve what, which information would be visible at which hierarchical level, where the bottlenecks would form that six months later would paralyze three departments.

The manager did not know this. Could not know it, because the interface had been designed precisely so that he would not know it, so that the complexity of the consequences would remain hidden behind the simplicity of the gesture.

The contemporary interface grants you the sensation of being able to do everything with a few gestures, including what you do not understand, especially what you do not understand. And this illusion of control breeds the most dangerous form of incompetence: the kind that confuses ease of use with mastery of the system, and discovers the difference only when the consequences are already irreversible.

If you have ever configured an automation in Jira, an approval flow in ServiceNow, a pipeline in Salesforce, with the feeling that you had everything under control: you were that manager. We all were.

## The epistemic heist

This is where the argument changes scale. The interface that hides complexity does more than cause individual errors by individual managers: it redefines which forms of knowledge the organization recognizes as legitimate. What the system records exists; what the system ignores disappears. And this operation has a precise name.

Organizational information systems, the entire apparatus of tools, processes, metrics, and methodological frameworks that govern contemporary work, perpetrate a systematic form of epistemic injustice. They privilege explicit, codifiable, quantifiable knowledge and marginalize everything that resists this reduction: tacit knowledge, situated knowledge, relational knowledge, the kind that lives in the hands of the experienced craftsperson, in the intuition of the professional who "senses" an anomaly before being able to prove it, in the oral memory of a department that knows how things actually work beyond what the procedures prescribe.

Miranda Fricker, in her foundational work on epistemic injustice, showed how certain people are systematically denied credibility as knowing subjects because their listeners lack the interpretive resources to recognize their competence. My argument extends this insight from people to forms of knowledge: in contemporary organizations, entire ways of knowing suffer a credibility deficit, those that cannot be captured in a database field, that do not produce a measurable indicator, that find no place in any dashboard.

The consequences are three, and each one compounds the others.

The first is the loss of organizational memory: what the system does not record ceases to exist for the system, and since the system has become the sole legitimate repository of collective memory, it ceases to exist for everyone.

The second is cognitive impoverishment: competencies that are not exercised because the system has made them redundant will atrophy, according to a biological law that no technological innovation has ever repealed.

The third, and perhaps the most paradoxical, is the reduction in the effectiveness of the systems themselves, which end up governing a representation increasingly distant from the reality they claim to represent. A map that, through successive simplifications, has lost all correspondence with the territory. And nobody notices, because by now everyone is looking at the map.

---

## Open the hood

The alternative exists, and it is the exact opposite of what the organizational software industry is selling.

It consists in rethinking tools as epistemic devices: systems designed to amplify human judgment rather than replace it, to make complexity legible rather than bury it, to document the process through which decisions are made, with all the uncertainties, negotiations, and trade-offs that every real decision entails.

In practice: an information system designed as an epistemic device would record the dissent voiced during a meeting, the expert's doubt that no dashboard captures, the reasons why a decision was made one way and not another. It would function as an organizational radio telescope, an instrument that amplifies weak signals and makes them readable, rather than as a digital assembly line that accepts only what has already been reduced to the expected format.

The difference is political before it is technical. A system that records only answers is an instrument of control. A system that also records the questions, the doubts, the discarded alternatives, is an instrument of knowledge.

Where there is a system, there is a margin. Where there is explicit knowledge, there is tacit knowledge that the system ignores and that, precisely because it is ignored, continues to operate undisturbed, as all forces do when we are careless enough not to name them.

The cartography of shadow zones is an act of care. And an act of resistance.
